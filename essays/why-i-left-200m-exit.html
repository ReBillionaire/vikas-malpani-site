Why I Left a $200M Exit to Start Again at Zero

Vikas Malpani | March 2026 | 8 min read

In 2016, CommonFloor merged with Quikr in a deal valued at $200 million. I was 34 years old. By most definitions, I had "made it." But something strange happened the day the deal closed: I felt nothing.

Not relief. Not joy. Not the rush of crossing some imaginary finish line. Just a strange emptiness, followed almost immediately by a question that wouldn’t leave me alone: What now?

The conventional answer was obvious. Take a year off. Travel. Maybe do some angel investing from a beach in Goa. A lot of founders who exit take that path, and I don’t judge them for it. Building a company from scratch to a $200M outcome over nearly a decade is exhausting in ways that are impossible to explain to someone who hasn’t done it.

But I couldn’t do it. And understanding why I couldn’t do it has been the most important lesson of my career.

The Obsession Doesn’t Stop

When we started maxHeap and built CommonFloor.com in 2007, India’s real estate market was completely opaque. Buyers had no data. Brokers controlled everything. The idea that you could search for a flat online, compare prices, read reviews of builders — that was science fiction.

We didn’t build CommonFloor because we ran a market analysis and found a "TAM opportunity." We built it because the problem was so obviously broken that we couldn’t not try to fix it. That obsession — that inability to look away from a broken system — is what carried us through years of near-zero revenue, through fundraising rejections, through the grind of building a two-sided marketplace city by city.

Here’s what nobody tells you about exits: the deal closes, but the obsession doesn’t stop. If you’re wired to see broken systems and feel compelled to fix them, a bank balance doesn’t change that wiring. It just frees you to chase even bigger problems.

The Three Questions

After CommonFloor, I found myself asking three questions every morning:

01

What’s the biggest unsolved problem I can see right now?

02

Am I uniquely positioned to solve it?

03

Will I regret not trying in ten years?

These questions led me to Leher — a live audio-video social platform that pioneered real-time dialogue in India. And then to Garvik AI, where we’re building ReBillion.ai — enterprise AI middleware that solves the knowledge infrastructure problem that every large organization struggles with.

Each company taught me something different. CommonFloor taught me how to build marketplaces. Leher taught me about the power (and fragility) of network effects in social products. ReBillion.ai is teaching me about the enterprise AI stack and how organizations actually adopt transformative technology.

Building for Meaning vs. Building for Money

The biggest shift after an exit isn’t financial. It’s psychological. When money is no longer the primary motivation, you’re forced to confront what actually drives you. And what I discovered is this: I build because building is how I think.

Companies are theories about the world made tangible. CommonFloor was a theory that Indian real estate could be made transparent. Leher was a theory that Indians wanted to connect through live dialogue, not just text. ReBillion.ai is a theory that enterprises can unlock their trapped knowledge with the right AI middleware layer.

Some of these theories are right. Some are partially right. Some need to evolve. But the act of testing them — of building the thing and seeing if reality agrees with your hypothesis — that’s the work. That’s the meaning.

The exit wasn’t the end of the story. It was the point where the real story started — the one where you build because you choose to, not because you have to.

What I Tell Founders Who Ask

A lot of early-stage founders ask me: "What’s it like after you exit?" They expect me to talk about freedom, or wealth, or some sense of completion. Instead, I tell them three things:

First, the exit is not the goal. If the only reason you’re building is to sell, you’ll run out of energy long before you get there. The companies that reach significant outcomes are the ones where the founders would have built them even if there was no exit market.

Second, the next thing will be harder. After an exit, expectations are higher — from investors, from your team, from yourself. You’ve proven you can build something. Now the question is whether you can do it again, in a new domain, with new technology, against new competitors. The second time around, you can’t blame inexperience for your mistakes.

Third, start when it hurts. The best time to start your next company isn’t when you’ve "rested enough." It’s when you find a problem that bothers you so much that not working on it feels wrong. For me, that happened faster than I expected every time.

The AI Chapter

Today, at Garvik AI, we’re building ReBillion.ai because we see a gap in the enterprise AI landscape that nobody is adequately addressing. Every company wants to deploy AI. Almost none have the knowledge infrastructure to make it work. The models are impressive, but models without context are just expensive autocomplete.

This is the biggest problem I’ve ever worked on. Bigger than real estate transparency. Bigger than social audio. Enterprise AI infrastructure will determine which organizations thrive in the next decade and which become irrelevant. We’re building the middleware layer that makes the difference.

I didn’t leave a $200M exit to "start again at zero." I left because zero is where the most important work begins. The bank account isn’t zero. The reputation isn’t zero. The network isn’t zero. But the product is zero. The team is new. The problem is fresh. And that first-day feeling — the one where everything is possible and nothing is proven — that’s the feeling I chase.

It turns out, that feeling is worth more than any exit.
