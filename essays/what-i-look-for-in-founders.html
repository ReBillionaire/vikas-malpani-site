What I Look For in Founders (It’s Not What VCs Say)

Vikas Malpani | January 2026 | 9 min read

I’ve been investing in startups since before it was fashionable. The two investments I’m most proud of — Unacademy and Quikr (via CommonFloor) — both became unicorns. Here’s what the founders of those companies had in common, and why it’s different from what most VCs claim to look for.

Every VC has a framework. MEDDPICC. TAM-SAM-SOM. Team-Market-Product fit triangles. These frameworks are useful for analyzing deals, but they’re terrible at predicting which founders will build generational companies. The signal that matters most doesn’t fit on a slide deck.

The Obsession Test

The single most reliable predictor I’ve found is what I call the Obsession Test. It’s simple: Can the founder talk about the problem — not their solution, but the problem — for an hour without repeating themselves?

When I first met Gaurav Munjal before Unacademy, he didn’t pitch me on a platform or a business model. He talked about the fundamental brokenness of how India educates its young people. He described specific students he’d met. He quantified the problem in ways I’d never heard before. He was angry about it — not performatively angry, genuinely angry. The business plan came later. The obsession came first.

Founders who are obsessed with the problem — not the opportunity, not the market, not the exit — are the ones who persist through the years when nothing works. And in startups, there are always years when nothing works.

Three Things That Matter (And Three That Don’t)

What Matters

1. Taste in problems

Great founders pick problems that are big enough to matter, specific enough to solve, and urgent enough that customers will pay before the product is perfect. This is a form of taste — pattern recognition for which problems are worth a decade of your life.

2. Speed of learning

Every startup thesis is wrong on day one. What matters is how fast the founder updates their understanding. The best founders I’ve backed have this uncanny ability to absorb new information, discard old assumptions, and reconfigure their approach without losing conviction in the core mission.

3. Ability to attract talent

Show me a founder’s first five hires and I’ll tell you if the company will work. Great founders attract people who are unreasonably talented for the stage of the company. If someone who could work at Google or Goldman joins a six-person startup, the founder has something special.

What Doesn’t Matter (As Much As People Think)

1. The pitch deck

I’ve seen beautifully crafted decks from founders who couldn’t build. And I’ve seen one-pagers from founders who went on to create billions in value. The deck is marketing. I want to see the mind.

2. Pedigree

IIT/IIM helps, but it’s neither necessary nor sufficient. Some of the most effective operators I’ve backed came from backgrounds nobody would have predicted. What matters is what you’ve built, not where you studied.

3. The current numbers

At the seed stage, metrics are nearly meaningless. The sample size is too small. The product is too early. I’m investing in the founder’s judgment and the problem’s importance, not a hockey-stick chart based on three months of data.

The Unacademy Bet

When I invested in Unacademy, it wasn’t a company yet. It was a YouTube channel where Gaurav was uploading educational videos. The "metrics" were YouTube views. The "revenue" was zero. No VC framework would have flagged this as an investment opportunity.

But I saw three things: a founder who understood the education problem at a visceral level, a medium (video) that was about to explode in India with falling data costs, and a determination that bordered on the irrational. Gaurav was going to build this whether anyone invested or not. My money just let him do it faster.

That investment became one of the most valuable angel bets in Indian startup history. Not because I was smart about the market, but because I was right about the founder.

Advice I Give Every Founder I Back

I invest at the seed and pre-seed stage, which means I’m usually the first or second check. The relationship is more like a co-conspirator than a board member. Here’s what I tell every founder after I invest:

Talk to customers before you talk to investors. The biggest mistake seed-stage founders make is optimizing for fundraising instead of learning. Your next round should be a natural consequence of customer love, not a goal in itself.

Hire slowly, fire quickly. At the seed stage, every hire is a cultural decision. One wrong person in a five-person team doesn’t reduce output by 20% — it reduces it by 80%.

Build for the problem you see, not the market investors want. If your problem is real and urgent, the market will find you. If you’re building for a market thesis, you’ll pivot every time a new report comes out.

Be honest about what’s not working. The founders who fail aren’t the ones with bad ideas — they’re the ones who can’t admit when something isn’t working and change course. Intellectual honesty is the most underrated founder trait.

The best founders I’ve backed all share one trait: they would build their company even if nobody invested. The money accelerates the mission. It doesn’t create it.

What I’m Looking For Now

Today, I’m particularly excited about founders working on AI infrastructure (obviously — I’m building in this space myself), India-specific enterprise software, the intersection of AI and education, and climate technology. But the sector matters less than the founder.

If you’re building something that keeps you up at night, something you’d work on even if the market said it was too small or too hard or too early — I want to hear about it. Not your deck. Not your metrics. Tell me about the problem. That’s where the conversation starts.
