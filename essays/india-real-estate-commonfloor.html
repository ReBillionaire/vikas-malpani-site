India’s Real Estate Was Broken. We Fixed It With Technology.

Vikas Malpani | December 2025 | 11 min read

In 2007, if you wanted to buy a flat in Bangalore, you had exactly two options: walk into a broker’s office and trust whatever they told you, or spend weeks visiting every builder’s site office across the city. There was no data. No comparison. No transparency. Just opacity designed to maximize broker commissions.

That was the India we started maxHeap in. A country where real estate was the single largest financial decision most families would ever make — and they were making it almost completely blind.

The Problem Was Obvious. The Timing Wasn’t.

When we started maxHeap and built CommonFloor.com, India’s internet penetration was roughly 4%. Smartphones barely existed. The idea of searching for apartments online seemed as practical as ordering groceries through a fax machine.

But we could see something most people couldn’t: the gap between how Indians wanted to discover real estate and how they were forced to discover it was enormous. The information asymmetry was staggering. Brokers had all the information. Buyers had none. And in a market where a single flat could cost a family’s entire lifetime savings, that asymmetry wasn’t just inconvenient — it was unjust.

We started from a two-bedroom apartment in Bangalore. Three founders, no funding, and a conviction that the internet would eventually transform how India bought and sold property. The "eventually" part was the risk. We were building for a future that was at least three to five years away.

Building a Marketplace City by City

CommonFloor was a classic cold-start marketplace problem. Buyers only come if there are listings. Builders only list if there are buyers. The chicken-and-egg problem that kills most marketplace startups nearly killed us too.

Our approach was unglamorous but effective: we started with Bangalore and went deep before going wide. We personally met every major builder in the city. We physically verified listings. We created detailed property profiles with floor plans, amenities, and neighborhood information that no one else had bothered to compile.

The key insight was that the information itself was the product, not just the platform. Buyers weren’t looking for a marketplace. They were looking for truth. Accurate, verified, comparable information about the single most expensive thing they’d ever buy. If we could be the most trusted source of that information, the marketplace dynamics would follow.

What Tiger Global Saw

When Tiger Global invested in CommonFloor, they weren’t just betting on a real estate portal. They were betting on three macro trends converging in India: rapidly increasing internet penetration, a massive housing demand driven by urbanization, and a generational shift where young Indian buyers would demand transparency in everything — including property transactions.

Tiger’s investment gave us the fuel to expand from Bangalore to every major Indian city. We went from a Bangalore-only platform to a national one in under two years. The playbook was the same in every city: go deep on data quality, build trust through accuracy, and let word-of-mouth among buyers do the marketing.

By the time we were operating in 200+ cities with over 5 lakh active listings, CommonFloor had become the default first stop for anyone researching property in India. We’d done to real estate information what Google had done to web search: organized it, made it accessible, and made it trustworthy.

The Quikr Merger

In 2016, CommonFloor merged with Quikr in what was valued as a $200M all-stock deal, creating India’s most comprehensive classifieds platform. The merger wasn’t just a financial transaction — it was a strategic consolidation that combined CommonFloor’s deep real estate expertise with Quikr’s massive horizontal classifieds platform.

Mergers are complicated. This one was no exception. But the logic was sound: real estate is fundamentally a classifieds problem, and the future belonged to platforms that could offer comprehensive coverage rather than vertical-only solutions. The combined entity had the breadth and the depth to dominate.

Building CommonFloor taught me the most important lesson of my career: the hardest problems in technology aren’t technical. They’re about trust. Users don’t adopt technology — they adopt trustworthy technology.

Lessons From Scaling Across India

Lesson 1: India is not one market. What works in Bangalore doesn’t work in Jaipur. What works in Mumbai doesn’t work in Lucknow. Language, buying behavior, property types, price ranges, broker dynamics — everything varies city by city. National scale in India requires city-level localization. There are no shortcuts.

Lesson 2: Trust is your moat. In a market where every other listing was fraudulent or misleading, our insistence on verified, accurate data was our biggest competitive advantage. It was expensive and slow, but it built a brand that users trusted with their most important financial decision.

Lesson 3: Timing is everything, and patience is the bridge. We started in 2007 but didn’t hit real scale until 2012-2013, when smartphone adoption crossed a tipping point. Five years of building before the market caught up. If we hadn’t had the conviction (and the frugality) to survive those early years, we wouldn’t have been positioned when the wave hit.

Lesson 4: The team is the company. We scaled from 3 co-founders to a team of hundreds across India. Every culture decision we made in the first 20 hires echoed through the entire organization. The early team defined the company’s DNA — its values, work ethic, and standards for quality.

What CommonFloor Means for What I’m Building Now

CommonFloor taught me that the most valuable thing you can build is a system that makes opaque information transparent. Real estate was opaque. We made it transparent. Enterprise knowledge is opaque. At ReBillion.ai, we’re making it transparent.

The pattern is the same. The scale is different. Instead of making property information accessible to home buyers, we’re making organizational knowledge accessible to every employee through AI. Instead of 200 cities, we’re deploying across Banking, Manufacturing, Real Estate, and EdTech. The information asymmetry is different, but the fundamental mission is identical: use technology to give people access to the information they need to make better decisions.

Some founders start from scratch every time. I start from lessons. CommonFloor’s lessons — about trust, timing, localization, and the power of solving information asymmetry — are embedded in everything we build at Garvik AI. They’re the invisible architecture underneath ReBillion.ai.

India’s real estate was broken. We fixed it with technology. Enterprise knowledge is broken. We’re fixing that too.
