A sewage problem. A friend. An obsession that wouldn’t stop.

This is the story of how I got here. Not the polished version. The real one.

The Sewage Problem That Changed Everything

It was 2007. I was 23 years old, fresh out of VTU Bangalore with a degree in Computer Science.

I was living in a Bangalore apartment complex, and one afternoon, the sewage system overflowed. It was disgusting, obviously. But what struck me wasn’t the mess. It was what happened next: suddenly, 500 families in the building realized they didn’t know each other. We had no platform to communicate. We couldn’t coordinate a response. We were strangers living three feet apart.

I decided to go knock on some doors. I was looking for someone else who cared about this problem. And I found Sumit Jain—my school friend—living two blocks away. He’d been thinking about the same thing. Why should people pay hidden commissions to brokers? Why couldn’t you search for an apartment online like you could books on Amazon? Why did buying property in India feel like trading secrets on the black market?

That’s when we knew. India’s real estate was broken. Completely broken. And we couldn’t not try to fix it.

Sumit and I teamed up with Lalit Mangal, and we founded maxHeap Technologies. We called the product CommonFloor.

“Everybody has ideas. Obsession is what separates people who do something from people who don’t. We were obsessed.”

Building CommonFloor: From Zero to 200 Cities

Raising capital in 2007-2008 was brutal. Nobody believed in us. Real estate? Technology? The combination seemed insane. We bootstrapped at first. We had no money for marketing, so we printed flyers. When we ran out of printing budget, we manually penciled apartment block numbers on the back of old flyers and reused them. I’m not exaggerating. We were that hungry.

The cold start was a slog. We went building by building in Bangalore. Then city by city. We trained local teams to manually verify listings. We paid for every piece of data. It was expensive. It was slow. But it was real.

Then Accel Partners believed in us. Series A in 2009. Tiger Global came in for Series B in 2011. And suddenly, the floodgates opened. We raised $7.5 million in Series C, then $30 million in Series D, then another $10 million from Google Capital. By the end, we had raised $69.6 million across seven rounds.

The numbers were staggering. We went from zero to 500,000 active listings. We were getting 70 lakh monthly visitors—that’s 7 million people a month—looking for homes. We had mapped 10 million homes across 200 cities. We had changed how India bought and sold real estate.

On January 8th, 2016, we merged with Quikr in an all-stock deal valued at $200 million. Sumit, Lalit, and I each walked away within a year. I was 32 years old. I had a bank account most people only dream about. I had achieved what every founder says they want to achieve.

And when it happened, I felt nothing.

The Emptiness After the Exit

I remember the day the deal closed. There were celebrations. There were congratulations. There were photographs for LinkedIn. And there was this strange, hollow feeling that I didn’t know how to name.

I had just pulled off something that — on paper — was a complete success. We had built something at scale. We had made investors money. We had solved a real problem for millions of Indians. We had created value.

So why did I feel empty?

I got a lot of recognition after the exit. MIT Technology Review named me one of their TR35—the 35 innovators under 35. I was 28 when that award came through. BusinessWorld called me the Hottest Young Entrepreneur. The press wrote stories. The money was real.

But something inside me couldn’t rest. The kind of rest everyone seemed to be recommending. “Take a year off,” my friends said. “Travel. You’ve earned it.” And I tried. I really did. But the emptiness didn’t go away. If anything, it got worse.

Looking back now, I understand what happened. The exit was a ending to one story. But I wasn’t wired for endings. I was wired for beginnings.

The Pattern: Community + Technology

I realized something after CommonFloor: I was drawn to the same type of problem, over and over. Problems at the intersection of community and technology. Problems that required building something tangible.

In 2018, I co-founded Leher with Atul Jaju, a former Goldman Sachs executive with 12 years of experience in financial systems. Atul brought rigor. I brought obsession. Together, we built a live audio-video social networking platform—think of it as a place where Indians could have real-time conversations about ideas, politics, culture, anything. We believed in “communities over metrics.”

We didn’t chase vanity numbers. We built features that mattered. Leher got picked up by the Google for Startups Accelerator in 2020. We raised funding from Orios Venture Partners. We were pioneering drop-in audio rooms—literally months before Clubhouse exploded onto the scene. Nobody knew it then, but we were ahead of the wave.

The problem was simple: Clubhouse got to scale faster. They had Silicon Valley hype. We were in India. By the time the market understood what live audio was about, there were a dozen global competitors. Leher exited in 2022.

But Leher taught me something crucial: what matters isn’t always about the market size. Sometimes the most important thing is that you believed in the community first. You built something real. And even if it didn’t become the next Unicorn, you moved the needle.

The Current Obsession: Enterprise AI

In 2023, Atul and I started Garvik AI. And now we’re building ReBillion.ai.

The problem we’re solving is one that nobody is talking about, but every large enterprise is struggling with: the knowledge infrastructure gap. Companies have data everywhere. Scattered across systems. Trapped in spreadsheets and emails and PDFs. They want to deploy AI. They want Copilots. They want to make their knowledge searchable. But almost none of them have the infrastructure to make it work.

Models are impressive. They’re also expensive, and without the right context layer, they’re just sophisticated autocomplete. We’re building the middleware. We’re building Rio and Ahaan—AI assistants that understand your company’s actual knowledge, not some generic training data.

We’re working with banks. Manufacturing companies. Real estate businesses. EdTech platforms. And the pattern is always the same: once you give them a knowledge layer that actually works, they can’t imagine going back.

This is the same pattern as CommonFloor. It’s the same pattern as Leher. A broken system. A clear problem. Technology as the solution. And the obsession that won’t let me stop working on it.

The only difference is the scale of the problem. Enterprise AI infrastructure will determine which organizations win in the next decade and which become irrelevant. This isn’t real estate. This isn’t social media. This is fundamental infrastructure. This is how companies think.

Backing Founders: The Privilege of Knowing What Works

In December 2015, before CommonFloor had even exited, I made an angel investment in Unacademy. The seed round was $586,000. I was investing alongside Sujeet Kumar from Flipkart, Aprameya Radhakrishna from Taxi4Sure, Phanindra Sama from RedBus, and my co-founder Sumit Jain.

I wasn’t doing this because I had studied the education market and decided it was attractive. I was doing it because I believed in the founder. He had the obsession. He saw an obvious problem—millions of Indians couldn’t afford quality education—and he couldn’t look away from it.

Unacademy became a unicorn in 2020. It’s now one of India’s most valuable EdTech companies. That investment turned into something meaningful, not just financially, but because it meant I got to be part of something that mattered.

I’ve made other bets. NxtWave—I invested $2.8 million in their pre-Series A in 2021. Phyllo. SeekAxis. FiveS Digital. Each one was because I saw a founder who couldn’t stop thinking about a problem.

I speak at FICCI. At CyFy. At TiE events. I mentor at IIT, IIM, and NIT. I spoke at Babson College in Boston. And every time someone asks me about investing, I tell them the same thing:

“Founders succeed because they want to succeed and ignore everything else. Market size doesn’t matter if the founder isn’t obsessed. TAM size doesn’t matter if the founder can’t see the problem clearly. What matters is: can this person not stop thinking about this problem?”

What I’ve Learned

I published an essay in India Business Journal with three words that have stayed with me ever since: “Think Long Term, Act Short Term.”

It’s a principle that runs through everything I do. You have to see far enough ahead to know you’re solving something real. A problem that matters in five years, in ten years. Something structural, not a trend. But then you have to move fast. You have to build. You have to ship. You have to learn from reality, not from your own assumptions.

The companies that succeed aren’t the ones that are obsessed with being market-driven. They’re the ones that are obsessed with the problem. Common misconception in startup land: you have to chase the market. You don’t. You have to chase the problem. If you solve the problem, the market will find you.

When it comes to investing, I’ve learned that founder quality beats market size every single time. I’d rather back an exceptional founder in a “small” market than a mediocre founder in a billion-dollar market. The exceptional founder will expand the market. The mediocre founder will shrink it.

On AI, I believe the real opportunity isn’t in the models. Models are becoming commodities. Everyone will have access to GPT-4, Claude, whatever comes next. The opportunity is in infrastructure. It’s in the layer that sits between the raw model and the business problem. It’s in knowledge management, in context retrieval, in making AI actually useful to real organizations.

And on India? I believe the next decade belongs to Indian founders who are solving India-scale problems. Not American founders who are trying to build American startups in India. Indian founders with global ambitions. Building products that can scale to 300 million users. Solutions that work for emerging markets first, then global markets.

India doesn’t need to copy Silicon Valley. India needs to build the future in its own way.

Why I Keep Starting Over

People ask me this all the time: why do you keep building? Why not just invest in startups and sit on your wealth?

The honest answer is: I don’t know how to not build. Building is how I think. Startups are theories about the world that I test by making them real. When the theory works, great. When it doesn’t, I learn why. And then I move to the next theory.

CommonFloor was a theory that Indian real estate could be transparent. It worked. Leher was a theory that Indians wanted to connect through live dialogue. It partially worked. It was ahead of its time, but the insight was right. ReBillion.ai is a theory that enterprises can unlock their trapped knowledge if they have the right infrastructure layer. I’m still testing this one.

The point is: I build because building is the only way I know how to be sure if something is true or not. You can talk about markets. You can analyze TAMs. You can make 50-slide pitch decks. But until you build it and put it in front of real people, you’re just guessing.

I’m tired of guessing. I’d rather build and know.

“The exit wasn’t the finish line. It was the point where I realized I was never running toward a finish line at all. I was running because I couldn’t not run.”

The obsession is the point. Not the exit. Not the money. Not the recognition. The obsession is the point.

That’s why I still build. That’s why I still back founders. That’s why I still believe that the next decade of technology will be shaped by Indian founders solving India-scale problems. Because the obsession is what separates people who see problems from people who fix them.
